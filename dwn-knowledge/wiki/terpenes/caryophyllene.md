**SUMMARY:** β-Caryophyllene (BCP) is the most abundant sesquiterpene in cannabis and the only dietary cannabinoid beyond the cannabis genus — a selective CB2 agonist. Above 0.5% it delivers potent anti-inflammatory and analgesic effects with no psychoactivity or sedation, effective for both inflammatory and neuropathic pain. **ARTICLE:**

## What It Is

β-Caryophyllene (BCP) is a bicyclic sesquiterpene and the most common sesquiterpenoid in cannabis (Mediavilla and Steinemann, 1997). It is also found in black pepper (*Piper nigrum*), copaiba balsam (*Copaifera officinalis*), and lavender. Its aroma is spicy, woody, and peppery — the "kick" note behind many heavy cannabis cultivars.

BCP is one of the top five most abundant terpenes in cannabis globally (Liktor-Busa et al., 2021). Being a sesquiterpene (15-carbon compound vs. 10-carbon monoterpenes), it is less volatile than myrcene or limonene — meaning it survives storage better and concentrates more during extraction. Extracts and concentrates routinely carry higher BCP % relative to flower.

## Threshold Data

- **<0.1%** — Aroma only. Spicy, peppery note; no pharmacological effect expected.
- **>0.1%** — Contributing. Mild anti-inflammatory baseline; background CB2 engagement.
- **>0.5%** — Noticeable. Anti-inflammatory and analgesic effects clinically relevant. For pain and inflammation goals, this is the target floor.
- **>1%** — Dominant. Full CB2-driven anti-inflammatory profile. Combined with myrcene and humulene, this is the pain-relief-profile stack.

## Effect Goals Served

**Primary:** pain (inflammatory and neuropathic), inflammation, body-relief-daytime
**Secondary:** daytime-anxiety (non-sedating CB2 anxiolytic/antidepressant), nausea, metabolic inflammation
**Not served:** sleep (no sedation mechanism), focus, social, creativity (no CNS uplift)

## Pharmacology

**CB2 Agonist — The Defining Feature:** BCP is the first proven phytocannabinoid beyond the cannabis genus — a selective full agonist at CB2 at 100 nM (Gertsch et al., 2008). CB2 receptors are expressed primarily in immune tissue, peripheral nervous system, and GI tract — not centrally like CB1. This means BCP produces no psychoactivity, no impairment, and no sedation. LaVigne et al. (2021) confirmed this directly: β-caryophyllene does not induce the CB1 tetrad behaviors (antinociception, hypothermia, catalepsy, hypolocomotion at CB1 doses). It is functionally non-intoxicating.

> **CB2 controversy — flagged:** Santiago et al. (2019) and Finlay et al. (2020) found no CB2 activity by BCP in their assay systems (measuring potassium currents/K⁺ channel hyperpolarization). Liktor-Busa (2021) also notes evidence for BCP as a *negative allosteric CB2 modulator* rather than orthosteric agonist. This is an active debate. The practical pharmacology is unaffected — anti-inflammatory and analgesic effects attributed to CB2-dependent mechanisms are consistently replicated across independent labs and reversed by CB2 antagonists. The binding mode is contested; the downstream effects are not.

**Anti-Inflammatory:** Anti-inflammatory via PGE-1 inhibition, comparable in potency to phenylbutazone (Basile et al., 1988) and on par with etodolac and indomethacin in an EO model (Ozturk and Ozbek, 2005). Also acts via NF-κB, TNF-α, and IL-6 pathways (Liktor-Busa et al., 2021) and as a PPAR-α agonist (Wu et al., 2014). **Critically: gastric cytoprotective** (Tambe et al., 1996) — unlike NSAIDs, BCP does not damage the stomach lining. This is its primary clinical differentiator vs. ibuprofen-class anti-inflammatory drugs.

**Analgesic — Broad Spectrum:** Effective for both inflammatory AND neuropathic pain (Klauke et al., 2014) — a wider analgesic profile than limonene (inflammatory/mechanical only). Oral trans-caryophyllene attenuates acute and chronic pain in mice (Paula-Freire et al., 2014). Peripheral cannabinoid and opioid receptors both involved (Katsuyama et al., 2013). Effective against antiretroviral-induced neuropathy (Aly et al., 2019) and paclitaxel-induced peripheral neuropathy (Segat et al., 2017).

**Anxiolytic / Antidepressant:** CB2-dependent anxiolytic and antidepressant effects in mice (Bahi et al., 2014). Non-sedating — no locomotion suppression, no impairment. This makes it the functional anxiety option for patients who cannot tolerate sedation (daytime pain patients, high-tolerance users).

**Neuroprotection:** Suppresses neuroinflammation in multiple sclerosis model (Alberti et al., 2017); stroke protection via microglial polarization (Tian et al., 2019); attenuates neuropathic pain and depression in diabetic mice (Flores-Soto et al., 2019).

**Addiction:** Russo (2011) notes CB2 is expressed in dopaminergic neurons in the nucleus accumbens; synthetic CB2 agonists inhibit DA release and cocaine self-administration. BCP as a selective CB2 agonist may have addiction treatment applications — early evidence only.

**What amplifies it:** CBD, humulene, myrcene (for pain stacking).
**What counteracts it:** Nothing directly counteracts BCP's mechanism — it is additive with most profiles and orthogonal to the sedation axis.

## Consumption Route

**Inhalation (flower, vape, concentrate):** Primary route. As a sesquiterpene, BCP is *less volatile* than monoterpenes — it survives storage and handling better, and is less degraded in pre-rolls than myrcene or limonene. CB2 is peripheral/immune and does not require blood-brain barrier crossing, so pulmonary absorption is effective for systemic anti-inflammatory reach.

**Concentrates:** Strongly preferred for BCP therapeutic goals. Sesquiterpenoids increase as a relative proportion during extraction (Russo, 2011). A 75%+ THC concentrate from a BCP-rich cultivar will carry meaningfully more BCP per dose than flower. For chronic pain patients, concentrates deliver more BCP per dollar.

**Oral / Edibles:** BCP is acid-sensitive and has **low oral bioavailability** (Liktor-Busa et al., 2021) — encapsulation improves this. Paula-Freire (2014) confirmed oral trans-caryophyllene has pain effects, suggesting some GI/hepatic CB2 activity survives first-pass. Crucially: BCP oxidizes to caryophyllene oxide under heat and UV (Booth et al., 2017) — **cooking destroys active BCP**. Edibles made with unencapsulated BCP extract or improperly processed oil will have reduced active BCP and more oxide. For pain goals, do not rely on standard edibles to deliver BCP effect — prioritize inhalation or tincture formats.

**Stability advantage:** More stable in properly stored flower than monoterpenes; less degraded with age. COA date matters less for BCP than for limonene or myrcene.
