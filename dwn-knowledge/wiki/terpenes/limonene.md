**HEADLINE:** Limonene is the daytime-anxiety terpene — citrus-forward mood lift powered by serotonin, not sedation.

**SUMMARY:** Limonene is a cyclic monoterpene in citrus and cannabis, the second most abundant terpenoid in nature. Above 0.5% it produces anxiety reduction and mood lift via serotonin pathways. The signature terpene for daytime-anxiety and social profiles. Pain relief is real but limited to inflammatory and mechanical models; inhalation is optimal.

**ARTICLE:**

## What It Is

D-limonene is a cyclic monoterpene and the second most widely distributed terpenoid in nature (Noma and Asakawa, 2010). It dominates citrus peel — lemon, orange, grapefruit — and is a major component in many cannabis chemovars, where it can constitute up to 77% of the monoterpenoid fraction in some cultivars (de Meijer, pers. comm. in Russo, 2011). Its aroma is unmistakably bright citrus — the smell people associate with "lemon kush" or "orange" strains.

Limonene is one of the three most globally abundant cannabis terpenes alongside myrcene and α-pinene (Liktor-Busa et al., 2021). It co-dominates with myrcene in many indica-leaning chemovars and is more pronounced in sativa-leaning profiles.

## Threshold Data

- **<0.1%** — Aroma only. Bright citrus note detectable; no pharmacological effect expected.
- **>0.1%** — Contributing. Mild mood uplift; citrus aroma becoming prominent.
- **>0.5%** — Noticeable effects. Anxiety reduction and mood lift distinctly present regardless of indica/sativa label. If the COA shows limonene >0.5%, plan for a daytime/social experience.
- **>1%** — Dominant. Strong mood-elevating, clear-headed profile. Combined with α-pinene, this produces the most functionally alert cannabis experience available.

*Note: Thresholds applied from DWN standard framework against anxiety/mood literature. Not directly established in source papers at cannabis COA percentages.*

## Effect Goals Served

**Primary:** daytime-anxiety, social, creativity
**Secondary:** inflammation, pain (inflammatory and mechanical models only), mood/depression
**Not served:** sleep (limonene above 0.5% is counterproductive for sedation goals — it is the opposing terpene to high-myrcene profiles), focus (minor contribution only)

## Pharmacology

**Anxiolytic / Antidepressant:** The most clinically meaningful finding for Talk to Ziggy. Citrus fragrance in ambient air produced normalization of Hamilton Depression Scores, successful antidepressant discontinuation in 9/12 patients, and CD4/8 immune ratio normalization (Komori et al., 1995). Lemon oil vapor reduced stress via serotonin (5-HT) and dopamine (DA) modulation in mice (Komiya et al., 2006; Fukumoto et al., 2008). Anti-anxiety effect mediated via 5-HT1A agonism (Russo et al., 2005). This is the terpene-level mechanism for why citrus-forward cannabis feels mentally lighter and socially easier.

**Adenosine A2A Receptor:** D-limonene is a direct agonist at adenosine A2A receptors (Park et al., 2011; confirmed LaVigne et al., 2021). A2A activation modulates dopamine signaling and contributes to cannabimimetic activity independent of CB1/CB2. Not a CB1/CB2 agonist — confirmed negative for direct cannabinoid receptor activity (Santiago et al., 2019; Finlay et al., 2020).

**Analgesic (Inflammatory and Mechanical Pain):** Effective at ≥50 mg/kg oral/IP against formalin-induced pain, mechanical allodynia, and acidic saline-induced muscle pain (do Amaral et al., 2007; Araújo-Filho et al., 2017). TNF-α and IL-1β reduction in neuropathic model (Piccinelli et al., 2017). However, **ineffective against thermal pain (hot-plate test)** and limonene produced local nociception when injected directly into paw tissue (Kaimoto et al., 2016). This makes limonene a targeted anti-inflammatory analgesic — for neuropathic or deep muscle pain, caryophyllene + myrcene outperform it.

**A note on sedation:** Do Vale et al. (2002) showed limonene increased barbiturate sleep time in rodents at high oral doses — the same result as myrcene. This is a pharmacological-dose animal model finding and does not reflect consumer inhalation. All human-relevant evidence indicates mood elevation and anxiety reduction. At cannabis-typical inhalation concentrations, limonene is a daytime terpene.

## Entourage Combinations

| Partner | Effect | Why |
|---|---|---|
| α-Pinene | Daytime-anxiety profile | Both cognition-positive; pinene's bronchodilation + limonene's serotonin uplift = focused, calm, clear |
| Low myrcene (<0.3%) | Daytime-anxiety profile | Trace myrcene adds body without sedation; pinene counteracts myrcene's heaviness |
| Linalool | Deeper anxiolytic layer | Anticonvulsant together (Jones et al., 2010); linalool's GABAergic calming + limonene's serotonergic uplift |
| Caryophyllene | Anti-inflammatory stack | CB2 + A2A = distinct anti-inflammatory pathways; good for pain-adjacent anxiety |
| High myrcene (>0.5%) | Mostly counteracted | Myrcene's sedation dominates; limonene's uplift is reduced though not fully eliminated |

**What amplifies it:** α-Pinene, linalool, CBD (Russo, 2011 — CBD + limonene + linalool noted as anxiolytic combination).
**What counteracts it:** High myrcene, high terpinolene (different CNS profile dominates).

## Consumption Route

**Inhalation (flower, vape, concentrate):** Best route. 70% human pulmonary uptake (Falk-Filipsson et al., 1993). Rapid onset — peak plasma within 30 minutes. Mood-elevating and anxiolytic effects documented via inhalation/aromatherapy in human studies. Inhaled limonene is excreted largely unchanged in exhaled air (minimal urinary detection).

**Oral / Edibles:** Better oral bioavailability than myrcene — 43% in rats; metabolites detected within 0.7 hours in humans (Schmidt and Göen, 2017). However, metabolites (perillic acid, terpineol, limonene-1,8-diol) are distinct compounds with their own pharmacology. The 5-HT/DA mood effects are documented via inhalation; oral limonene's CNS effect profile is less established. Anti-inflammatory/pain effects were demonstrated at high oral doses in animals — some oral pain activity is plausible. For anxiety and mood goals, prioritize inhalation formats.

**Degradation:** Limonene is a monoterpene that oxidizes readily. Old or poorly stored flower loses limonene quickly. Always check COA date — pre-roll values are approximate.
