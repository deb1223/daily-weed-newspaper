**HEADLINE:** Ocimene is the sweet-herbal freshness terpene — highly volatile, antifungal, and the light-heady note behind the social-creativity stack.

**SUMMARY:** β-Ocimene is the sweetest, most volatile cannabis monoterpene — delivering the fresh herbal sweetness note in tropical and fruity cultivars. Antifungal activity is well-documented; anti-inflammatory and antiviral properties are actively emerging. Not covered in the three primary DWN source papers. Above 0.5%, it completes the social-creativity stack alongside terpinolene and limonene.

**ARTICLE:**

> **Source note:** (Z)-β-Ocimene is confirmed as a cannabis monoterpene (M12) in Booth & Bohlmann (2019) Figure 2 — a dedicated review of terpene biosynthesis in *Cannabis sativa* — and appears in Liktor-Busa et al. (2021) Figure 1. Neither paper covers ocimene pharmacology; LaVigne et al. (2021) did not test it. Pharmacology below draws on broader published literature with appropriate caveats.

## What It Is

β-Ocimene is an acyclic (open-chain) monoterpene — no ring structure, making it structurally distinct from pinene (bicyclic), terpinolene (monocyclic), and limonene (monocyclic). Booth & Bohlmann (2019) Figure 2 specifically identifies **(Z)-β-ocimene** (the cis isomer, M12) as the cannabis biosynthetic product — produced from geranyl diphosphate (GPP) by mono-TPS enzymes. Liktor-Busa et al. (2021) Figure 1 also lists ocimene in the acyclic monoterpene category alongside β-myrcene and α-phellandrene as monoterpenes "commonly encountered in cannabis." Both isomers ((Z)/cis and (E)/trans) may be detected in cannabis samples; the characterized biosynthesis product is the (Z) form.

Its aroma is distinctively sweet, herbal, fresh, and slightly woody with floral undertones — the "sweetness" note that distinguishes fruity and tropical cannabis cultivars from the piney, citrus, or earthy profiles of other terpene combinations. Found in: parsley, basil, mint, orchids, mangoes, and a wide range of flowering plants in addition to cannabis.

In cannabis, ocimene appears alongside terpinolene in many Haze-derived and tropical cultivars: Strawberry Cough, Dutch Treat, Golden Pineapple, J1, Space Queen, Papaya (sativa-type), and various fruity hybrids. It is typically a secondary or tertiary terpene rather than the dominant; its effect contribution is primarily through the social-creativity entourage combination.

**Volatility — the critical practical issue:** β-Ocimene has one of the lowest boiling points of cannabis terpenes (~65°C). This means it evaporates faster than almost any other terpene during:
- Drying and curing (significant ocimene loss before the consumer ever touches the product)
- Storage (volatile loss continues in stored flower)
- Combustion (essentially all ocimene lost in a lit joint before inhalation)
- High-temperature vaporization

**Live resin and fresh frozen products preserve ocimene that cured flower never will.** This is not a minor distinction — it may mean that "ocimene as it exists in the living plant" is largely unavailable in standard dried flower formats. COA data from cured flower likely underrepresents the original terpene profile significantly for ocimene specifically. Booth & Bohlmann (2019) confirm this directly: "variable quantitative losses of terpenes, especially the more volatile monoterpenes, may be another cause of terpene variation" when dried plant material is analyzed.

## Threshold Data

- **<0.1%** — Aroma only. Sweet, herbal note; no pharmacological effect expected.
- **>0.1%** — Contributing. Antifungal baseline; mild contribution to overall terpene profile.
- **>0.5%** — Noticeable. Antifungal and anti-inflammatory effects may be pharmacologically relevant. Completing the social-creativity terpene stack at this level.
- **>1%** — Dominant. Full ocimene character expressed. Rare in cured flower; more achievable in live resin and fresh frozen extracts.

*Note: Thresholds applied from DWN standard framework. Source papers do not establish these thresholds directly. Ocimene pharmacology data comes from broader literature, not the three primary DWN papers.*

## Effect Goals Served

**Primary:** social/creativity profile (as the third pillar alongside terpinolene and limonene), antifungal (topical)
**Secondary:** anti-inflammatory (mild, less robust than caryophyllene or myrcene), antiviral (emerging, early evidence)
**Not served:** sleep (no sedative data), pain (insufficient analgesic literature), focus (pinene is the memory/focus terpene), heavy anxiety (insufficient anxiolytic data)

## Pharmacology

> *All citations in this section are from broader literature outside the three primary DWN source papers. Liktor-Busa et al. (2021) identifies ocimene as a common cannabis terpene (Figure 1) but does not cover its pharmacology.*

**Antifungal — Most Consistently Documented:** β-Ocimene demonstrates antifungal activity in multiple independent studies, including against *Candida albicans* and dermatophytes (various EO studies, 2005–2020). This is ocimene's pharmacologically best-established property, consistent across different research groups. Clinically relevant primarily for topical formulations (transdermal products, salves) where direct antimicrobial contact occurs. Less relevant for inhalation-based consumption where systemic antifungal concentrations are not achieved.

**Anti-Inflammatory (Moderate Evidence):** Studies on ocimene-containing essential oils show reduction in TNF-α and IL-6 production in LPS-stimulated macrophage models, consistent with NF-κB pathway modulation. Effect size is substantially weaker than caryophyllene's CB2-mediated anti-inflammatory activity or myrcene's opioid-pathway analgesia. The anti-inflammatory mechanism for ocimene specifically is not well-characterized — NF-κB suppression is the proposed pathway by analogy with related monoterpenes.

**Antiviral (Emerging Evidence):** Post-2020 research into plant-derived antivirals has identified β-ocimene among terpenes showing in vitro antiviral activity. Evidence is early-stage and in vitro only — no clinical or animal model data at cannabis-relevant concentrations. Included for completeness; not sufficient for a Talk to Ziggy recommendation based on antiviral goals.

**Plant Defense — Ecological Role:** β-Ocimene is a well-characterized herbivore-triggered plant volatile — released when plants are attacked as both a deterrent signal and a call for predators of the herbivores. This ecological role is the most extensively published aspect of ocimene biology. It is not directly pharmacologically relevant to cannabis consumers but explains why ocimene appears in such diverse plant species and why it appears in cannabis trichomes at all.

**Cannabimimetic — Not Tested:** Ocimene was not included in LaVigne et al. (2021). No CB1/CB2 binding data from the primary DWN source papers. The cannabinoid receptor interaction profile for ocimene is uncharacterized in the literature available to this project.

**Entourage Effect — Skepticism Required:** Booth & Bohlmann (2019) state that "no molecular mechanism has been demonstrated to explain a potential synergy of terpenes with cannabinoids" beyond β-caryophyllene's CB2 activity — and that in one rat study, terpenes were found not to contribute to cannabinoid-mediated analgesia [Rousseau & Sabol, 2018]. They suggest the placebo effect, partially mediated through the endocannabinoid system, may explain some perceived strain-specific terpene effects. This is an honest counterpoint to entourage claims. The social-creativity profile remains a useful framework for terpene-guided recommendations, but the mechanism is better understood as aroma-driven behavioral influence than demonstrated receptor pharmacology for ocimene specifically.

**Aroma Contribution — Functionally Important:** Ocimene's sweet, herbal, tropical character contributes meaningfully to the aromatic experience of social-creativity cultivars. While aroma is not pharmacology, the olfactory–limbic connection is real (see linalool's unique olfactory pain pathway), and ocimene's pleasant, mood-associated aroma likely contributes to set and setting effects in social cannabis use — a real phenomenon even if not yet quantified.

## Consumption Route

**Live Resin / Fresh Frozen (strongly preferred for ocimene goals):** The only reliable way to deliver meaningful ocimene concentrations is through live resin or fresh frozen extracts — products made from fresh, uncured cannabis that is flash-frozen immediately after harvest. Standard drying and curing loses a substantial fraction of ocimene before processing begins. If a patient specifically wants the social-creativity terpene profile and ocimene is part of the target, live resin or fresh frozen is the correct format recommendation.

**Low-Temperature Vaporization (second-best):** If flower is the format, low-temperature vaporization (160–165°C) preserves more ocimene than higher temperatures, though curing has already degraded much of it. Vaporization is strongly preferred over combustion.

**Combustion (pre-rolls, pipes):** Essentially no ocimene delivery. Between curing losses and combustion temperatures, virtually all ocimene is destroyed before it can reach the consumer. Pre-rolls are the worst format for any terpene goal; for ocimene specifically, they are functionally irrelevant.

**Oral / Edibles:** Ocimene is rapidly degraded in food-grade processing and in gastric environments. Oral delivery does not meaningfully deliver ocimene. Antifungal activity in GI context is possible with high-dose concentrated extracts, but not at cannabis edible concentrations.

**COA interpretation note:** Because ocimene degrades so rapidly, COA data on cured flower reflects a fraction of the original profile. A COA showing 0.3% ocimene on cured flower may represent a cultivar that had 0.8% or more at harvest. When the scraper begins populating `terpene_ocimene`, interpret values conservatively — and specifically query for live resin products when the goal is maximizing ocimene delivery.
