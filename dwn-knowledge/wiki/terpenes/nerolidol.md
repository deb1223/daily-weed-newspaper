## What It Is

Nerolidol (also called peruviol or perolivil) is an acyclic sesquiterpene alcohol — a 15-carbon open-chain compound with a hydroxyl group, placing it in the oxygenated sesquiterpene category. Liktor-Busa et al. (2021) Figure 1 classifies it alongside bisabolol and caryophyllene oxide as an oxygenated sesquiterpene, structurally distinct from the hydrocarbon sesquiterpenes β-caryophyllene and α-humulene. It exists as two natural isomers, cis-(Z) and trans-(E); both occur in cannabis, and the proportion of each can vary.

**An important biosynthetic nuance:** Booth & Bohlmann (2019) Figure 2 — the most comprehensive characterization of cannabis sesquiterpene TPS products to date — lists 14 characterized sesquiterpene structures (S1–S14) but nerolidol is not among them. In contrast, bisabolol and β-caryophyllene are explicitly listed. Booth & Bohlmann specifically note that "other terpene derivatives detected in cannabis may arise non-enzymatically due to oxidation or due to thermal- or UV-induced rearrangements during processing or storage." Nerolidol is chemically related to farnesol ((E)-β-farnesol = S2) and farnesene ((E)-β-farnesene = S3), both of which ARE listed Figure 2 products. This raises a meaningful possibility: some or all nerolidol detected in cannabis COA data may arise from non-enzymatic conversion of these acyclic farnesol/farnesene precursors, rather than direct CsTPS biosynthesis. This does not invalidate nerolidol's pharmacological activity — but it does mean COA values may be influenced by processing conditions (heat, oxidation, UV exposure during drying and curing) as well as the original plant genetics.

**Practical implication:** Unlike β-caryophyllene or α-humulene (both confirmed CsTPS products), nerolidol in cured cannabis may be partly artifactual. Two products from the same cultivar, processed differently, could have meaningfully different nerolidol readings on COA. This is a caution for scraper-populated `terpene_nerolidol` values — they require more contextual interpretation than the hydrocarbon sesquiterpene columns.

**Aroma:** Woody, floral, and waxy with rose and citrus-peel undertones. Russo (2011) describes it as found in "oranges, rose, neroli, lemon grass, and ginger." The aroma is more subtle and complex than the sharp piney/citrus monoterpenes — it contributes depth and roundness to the overall cannabis fragrance without dominating.

**Cannabis presence:** Nerolidol appears as a secondary or minor sesquiterpene across multiple cultivar types. It is not the defining terpene of any specific chemotype the way terpinolene defines Haze or myrcene defines indica-dominant cultivars. It tends to appear alongside caryophyllene and linalool in cultivars that lean toward the sedation/body-relief profile — Gelato genetics, Wedding Cake, some OG crosses, and strains with significant floral character.

## Threshold Data

- **<0.1%** — Aroma only. Woody, floral note; no pharmacological effect expected.
- **>0.1%** — Contributing. Sedative and anti-inflammatory baseline possible; skin-penetrant property active at any concentration for topical use.
- **>0.5%** — Noticeable. Sedative effects potentially pharmacologically relevant at this level alongside other sedation-axis terpenes (myrcene, linalool). Antinociceptive contribution may be active.
- **>1%** — Dominant. Full nerolidol sedation character. Uncommon in typical cured flower at this level; more achievable in some live resin or concentrate formats.

*Note: Thresholds applied from DWN standard framework. Russo (2011) and Liktor-Busa (2021) studies do not establish these thresholds directly at cannabis COA percentages. COA interpretation caveat re: non-enzymatic origin applies.*

## Effect Goals Served

**Primary:** sleep/sedation (synergistic with THC and CBN per Russo), anti-inflammatory (Liktor-Busa), antinociceptive (mild — Liktor-Busa)
**Secondary:** topical skin penetration enhancement (unique among cannabis terpenes — augments dermal absorption of other compounds in topical formulations)
**Not served:** focus (counterproductive — sedation axis), social/creativity (wrong profile — sedation opposes social-creative energy), daytime anxiety (sedation caution for anxiety patients)

**Unique angle — CBN synergy:** Russo explicitly identifies THC **and CBN** as synergistic cannabinoids for nerolidol's sedative properties. CBN is itself sedative and increasingly available as a specific product format (CBN tinctures, CBN-infused flower, CBN + THC sleep products). For a patient specifically seeking maximum sleep support, nerolidol-forward genetics combined with a CBN-enhanced product format is the most pharmacologically defensible recommendation from the primary DWN source literature.

## Pharmacology

> *Russo (2011) Table 2 — primary source. Liktor-Busa et al. (2021) Section B.4 — primary source for analgesic/anti-inflammatory and pharmacokinetics. LaVigne et al. (2021) — not tested. Booth & Bohlmann (2019) — not listed as characterized CsTPS product.*

**Sedation — Documented (Russo 2011):** Binet et al. (1972) established sedative properties for nerolidol. Russo's Table 2 entry specifically identifies THC and CBN as the synergistic cannabinoids — distinguishing nerolidol from most other terpenes whose sedative properties are listed with THC alone. This CBN synergy note is unique in Table 2 and has direct product format implications (see Effect Goals, CBN synergy above).

**Skin Penetrant — Documented (Russo 2011):** Cornwell and Barry (1994) established nerolidol as a skin penetrant — a compound capable of enhancing dermal permeation of other molecules. Russo explicitly draws the topical application conclusion: nerolidol "may augment cannabinoid absorption via topical application." This is nerolidol's most clinically distinctive property relative to other cannabis terpenes. No other primary DWN terpene has this skin-penetrant property documented in the source literature. For topical formulations (CBD salves, THC patches, pain creams), nerolidol's co-presence enhances delivery of active compounds across the skin barrier.

**Antimalarial — Potent (Russo 2011):** Lopes et al. (1999) and Rodrigues Goulart et al. (2004) documented potent antimalarial activity. Russo describes this as potentially useful "combined with cannabis derivatives" in endemic regions. This is nerolidol's most scientifically striking pharmacological property and the most extensively published non-sedative activity in the literature. It is not directly relevant to Las Vegas dispensary consumer goals, but it demonstrates that nerolidol is a pharmacologically potent compound — not merely an aromatic footnote.

**Anti-Leishmanial (Russo 2011):** Arruda et al. (2005) documented anti-leishmanial activity. Same context as antimalarial — pharmacologically significant, not consumer-facing.

**Antinociceptive and Anti-Inflammatory (Liktor-Busa 2021):** Liktor-Busa Section B.4 covers antinociceptive and anti-inflammatory activity based on Pinheiro et al. (2011), Khadabakhsh et al. (2015), Ogwumwade et al. (2019), and Fonseca et al. (2016). These studies used nociceptive and inflammatory animal models. Fonseca et al. (2016) investigated the mechanisms. This data supports nerolidol's inclusion in the anti-inflammatory stack, though its analgesic potency appears weaker than caryophyllene's CB2 mechanism or myrcene's opioid pathway.

**Cannabimimetic — Not Tested (LaVigne 2021):** Nerolidol was not included in LaVigne et al.'s five-compound study. No CB1/CB2 receptor binding data from primary DWN sources. The cannabinoid receptor interaction profile is uncharacterized in the literature available to this project. Given the sedative property documented by Russo with THC/CBN synergy, there is plausible reason to hypothesize endocannabinoid system involvement — but this remains unconfirmed.

## Pharmacokinetics

**Low Oral Bioavailability — Important Practical Consequence:** Liktor-Busa et al. (2021) notes that first-pass metabolism nearly eliminates nerolidol after oral administration, with a half-life of approximately 44 minutes in rats. Similar effects observed in rabbit models. Oral bioavailability is substantially lower than inhalation. This is directly relevant to product format recommendations — edibles are a poor delivery route for nerolidol's sedative and anti-inflammatory effects.

**Intranasal Absorption — Rapid and Effective:** The same pharmacokinetics section documents that intranasal application of nerolidol shows rapid onset and absorption in the rodent brain via GCMS confirmation — bypassing first-pass hepatic metabolism. By extension, inhalation (which delivers terpenes to the lung vasculature similarly to intranasal delivery) is expected to produce substantially better bioavailability than oral routes. This is consistent with the CLAUDE.md general principle favoring inhalation for terpene goals, but for nerolidol specifically, the PK data makes this more than a general preference — it is the recommended route.

## Consumption Route

**Inhalation (flower, vaporization — strongly preferred):** Pharmacokinetic data supports inhalation as the primary delivery route for nerolidol. Bypasses first-pass hepatic metabolism; rapid absorption. Low-temperature vaporization preserves the full sesquiterpene profile. For sleep goals, vaporization 30–60 minutes before bedtime with myrcene + linalool genetics is the optimal protocol.

**Oral / Edibles:** Poor delivery route for nerolidol specifically due to rapid first-pass elimination. Edibles with nerolidol-containing cannabis oil will deliver minimal nerolidol systemically. The other sleep-profile terpenes (myrcene via opioid pathway, linalool via GABA) are somewhat more orally bioavailable, meaning an edible's sleep effects will be driven by those mechanisms rather than nerolidol. If an edible format is required, recommend myrcene + linalool genetics (not nerolidol-specific).

**Topical:** Nerolidol's skin-penetrant property makes it uniquely valuable in topical formulations — not primarily for nerolidol's own effects, but as an absorption enhancer for CBD, THC, caryophyllene, and other active compounds in the formulation. This is the one context where nerolidol's oral bioavailability limitations are irrelevant.

**COA interpretation note:** Given the possibility that some nerolidol in cured flower arises from non-enzymatic processing rather than direct biosynthesis, COA values may vary significantly between batches of the same cultivar depending on drying and curing conditions. When `terpene_nerolidol` is populated, interpret values with this variability in mind — a low reading doesn't necessarily mean the cultivar's genetics are nerolidol-poor; it may reflect careful low-temperature processing that minimized oxidative conversion. This is the inverse of the ocimene problem: while ocimene is lost through heat, nerolidol may actually be *generated* by certain processing conditions.
