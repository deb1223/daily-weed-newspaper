## What It Is

α-Humulene (also called α-caryophyllene in older literature — confusing but chemically real) is a monocyclic sesquiterpene. As a sesquiterpene, it has a 15-carbon backbone biosynthesized from farnesyl diphosphate (FPP) rather than the 10-carbon GPP used by monoterpenes — making it structurally related to but distinct from the monoterpenes that dominate cannabis's volatile fraction. It has a single 11-membered ring structure.

Liktor-Busa et al. (2021) categorizes α-humulene as a monocyclic sesquiterpene hydrocarbon (Figure 1), distinct from β-caryophyllene (bicyclic). Both appear in the sesquiterpene column alongside nerolidol and bisabolol as the primary cannabis sesquiterpenes. Booth & Bohlmann (2019) confirm that α-humulene and β-caryophyllene co-occur in cannabis through the same CsTPS multi-product enzyme — a single synthase produces both sesquiterpenes simultaneously. This co-production is consistent across chemovars and is the primary reason these two terpenes are so reliably found together in cannabis.

**Aroma:** Earthy, woody, herbal, and slightly spicy — distinctly "hoppy" because α-humulene is the same compound responsible for the characteristic aroma of hops (*Humulus lupulus*). Found in hops, sage, ginseng, *Cordia verbenacea*, and various other aromatic plants. In cannabis, its earthy-woody character underpins the "OG," "diesel," and "Kush" aromatic profiles — the dank, complex base note of these classic chemotypes.

**Cannabis chemovars:** α-Humulene typically appears as a secondary sesquiterpene alongside β-caryophyllene. OG Kush lineage (OG Kush, Headband, Tahoe OG, SFV OG), Chemdawg genetics, and other "gas" or "diesel" cultivars consistently carry humulene alongside their dominant caryophyllene profile. It is rarely the leading terpene; it is almost always the reliable companion.

**Stability:** As a sesquiterpene, humulene is substantially more stable than cannabis monoterpenes. The higher boiling point and larger carbon backbone mean it survives standard drying and curing with much lower loss than ocimene, terpinolene, or limonene. COA data on cured flower is a more reliable reflection of actual humulene content than for volatile monoterpenes. This makes humulene one of the more consistent terpenes to track via COA — when the scraper populates `terpene_humulene`, those values should be relatively accurate for cured flower.

## Threshold Data

- **<0.1%** — Aroma only. Earthy, woody note; no pharmacological effect expected.
- **>0.1%** — Contributing. Anti-inflammatory baseline; co-occurring caryophyllene likely dominant effect contributor.
- **>0.5%** — Noticeable. CB1/CB2 cannabimimetic effects active at this level; anti-inflammatory and antinociceptive effects potentially pharmacologically relevant. This threshold marks humulene as an active entourage contributor.
- **>1%** — Dominant. Full humulene character expressed; uncommon as the leading terpene but achievable in OG Kush lineage products.

*Note: Thresholds applied from DWN standard framework. LaVigne et al. (2021) used 200 mg/kg i.p. in mice — dose-translation to cannabis COA percentages applies the standard framework, not a direct comparison from this study.*

## Effect Goals Served

**Primary:** pain relief (CB1 partial agonism + CB2 activation + PGE2 anti-inflammatory), inflammation (PGE2 pathway), body-relief-daytime profile (as part of caryophyllene + humulene + moderate pinene stack)
**Secondary:** mild cannabimimetic sedation/analgesia via A2a-mediated mechanisms (see Pharmacology — unique to humulene)
**Not served:** sleep (no GABAergic/opioid sedation data), focus (pinene is the focus terpene), social/creativity (terpinolene/limonene/ocimene stack), daytime anxiety (limonene/pinene stack)

## Pharmacology

> *LaVigne et al. (2021) directly tested α-humulene — one of only five terpenes in the study. This is primary DWN source data. Liktor-Busa et al. (2021) Section B.3 covers humulene pharmacology, drawing primarily on Pinheiro et al. (2011) and Basting et al. (2019). Russo (2011) does not cover humulene in Table 2.*

**Cannabimimetic — Directly Confirmed (LaVigne 2021):** α-Humulene induced all four classic cannabinoid tetrad behaviors in mice (analgesia, catalepsy, hypothermia, hypolocomotion) at 200 mg/kg i.p. — confirming cannabimimetic activity. Figure 1 shows moderate efficacy in the radar chart vs. the WIN55,212-2 positive control, below β-Pinene but above Geraniol and Linalool in several domains.

**CB1 Receptor — Partial Agonist (Orthosteric + Allosteric):** The tail flick assay (Figure 2A) showed that rimonabant (CB1 inverse agonist) partially reversed α-humulene analgesia — confirming CB1 partial mediation of the antinociceptive effect. CB1 ERK activation was confirmed in CB1-CHO cells and was rimonabant-sensitive (Figures 6A and 7A). Competition binding (Figure 8A) showed α-humulene with "some competition and semi-biphasic properties" against ³H-CP55,940 — suggesting both orthosteric and potential allosteric binding at CB1, similar to β-caryophyllene's CB2 binding profile complexity.

**CB2 Activation — Confirmed:** ERK phosphorylation in CB2-expressing cells confirmed (Figure S12) — consistent with the pattern for all five tested terpenes. This makes α-humulene one of the few cannabis terpenes with direct CB2 activation data, alongside β-caryophyllene's well-established CB2 agonism.

**Unique Mechanism — A2a Dominant Catalepsy:** The most pharmacologically distinctive finding for α-humulene in LaVigne et al. is in the catalepsy assay. Istradefyllene (A2a adenosine receptor antagonist) completely blocked α-humulene-induced catalepsy (Figure 4A), while rimonabant only partially blocked it. This is the only terpene in the five-compound study where A2a dominates a tetrad behavior so decisively. A2a partial reversal of hypothermia was also observed (Figure 3A). This multi-receptor mechanism (CB1 + A2a + independent pathways) may contribute to humulene's body-effect profile being qualitatively different from caryophyllene's purely CB2-mediated effects.

**Not via Opioid Pathway:** Humulene activated ERK in MOR-CHO cells, but naloxone (opioid antagonist) could not block this response (Figure S13B) — confirming ERK stimulation was not through the mu opioid receptor. LaVigne et al. conclude this supports receptor-specific CB1/CB2 interaction rather than opioid system engagement.

**No βarrestin2 Recruitment:** At any concentration tested, α-humulene showed no βarrestin2 recruitment at CB1 (Figure 8C). This is consistent with a bias or allosteric modulation pattern — engaging signaling pathways (ERK, cAMP) without recruiting arrestin. The cAMP inhibition was only observed at high micromolar concentrations (Figure 8B), unlike WIN55,212-2 which showed high potency full efficacy inhibition.

**Anti-Inflammatory — PGE2 Pathway (Liktor-Busa 2021):** Pinheiro et al. (2011) demonstrated that essential oil from *Cordia verbenacea* (high α-humulene content) reduced inflammatory markers including PGE2, TNF-α, and related cytokines in preclinical inflammation models. Basting et al. (2019) extended this work. These findings support anti-inflammatory activity via prostaglandin pathway modulation — a different mechanism from β-caryophyllene's CB2-mediated anti-inflammatory effects, suggesting additive potential when both are present (as they reliably are).

**Antinociceptive — EO Data with Caveats:** Liktor-Busa et al. (2021) notes antinociceptive activity in writhings test and formalin models using essential oils with high humulene content. Critical caveat: these studies use essential oils where multiple terpenes are present simultaneously — the effect cannot be attributed to humulene alone. Liktor-Busa explicitly acknowledges limited purified humulene pharmacology data. LaVigne's isolated compound data provides more mechanistically clean evidence for humulene's antinociceptive contribution.

**Anti-proliferative — Emerging, Dose Caveat Required:** Some studies have identified anti-proliferative properties in humulene-containing essential oils. The same dose caveat Booth & Bohlmann (2019) apply to all cannabis monoterpene anti-proliferative claims applies here: amounts required in preclinical models are excessively high relative to cannabis consumer exposures. Not sufficient for a therapeutic claim.

## Entourage Combinations

| Partner | Effect | Why |
|---|---|---|
| β-Caryophyllene | Pain-relief / anti-inflammatory (full stack) | Natural co-occurrence via same CsTPS enzyme; CB2 (BCP) + CB1/A2a (humulene) = dual-receptor analgesia + dual anti-inflammatory mechanism (PGE2 pathway + CB2-mediated) |
| Myrcene + Caryophyllene | Pain-relief profile (three-terpene stack) | Adding myrcene's opioid-pathway analgesia creates a three-mechanism pain stack: opioid (myrcene) + CB2 (BCP) + CB1/A2a (humulene) |
| Moderate Pinene | Body-relief-daytime profile | Humulene + BCP + moderate pinene = body relief without sedation; pinene counteracts sedative tendencies and adds bronchodilatory and memory-protective effects for daytime use |
| High Myrcene | **Caution for daytime goals** | Myrcene at >0.5% introduces sedation; acceptable for evening body-relief goals, but shifts the profile toward sedation — inconsistent with the body-relief-daytime profile |
| Linalool | Sleep + pain (evening) | Linalool's GABAergic mechanism adds sedation to the body-relief stack; appropriate for nighttime body-relief/sleep overlap |

**What amplifies it:** β-Caryophyllene (natural partner, dual-receptor coverage), moderate myrcene.
**What counteracts it (for body-relief goals):** High linalool or high myrcene (shifts from body-relief to sedating-indica profile).

## Consumption Route

**Inhalation (flower, vaporization):** Best delivery route for the full humulene terpene profile. Unlike the highly volatile monoterpenes (ocimene, terpinolene), humulene is a sesquiterpene with a higher boiling point and substantially better stability through drying and curing. Standard cured flower retains meaningful humulene content — COA data is a reliable guide.

**Edibles / Oral:** Sesquiterpenes generally have better oral bioavailability than monoterpenes, with lower metabolic conversion losses in the first pass. Liktor-Busa et al. note the *Cordia verbenacea* essential oil antinociceptive studies used oral administration in preclinical models, and effects were observed — suggesting oral route is viable for the anti-inflammatory mechanism. However, the CB1/CB2 mechanisms characterized by LaVigne et al. were via i.p. injection; oral bioavailability for the cannabimimetic pathway is less well characterized.

**Vaporization vs. Combustion:** Like all terpenes, low-temperature vaporization preserves more of the terpene profile than combustion. The larger concern here is less about humulene volatility (which is well below that of monoterpenes) and more about preserving the full caryophyllene + humulene dual sesquiterpene profile — which requires attention to vaporization temperature to avoid oxidative degradation of both.

**COA reliability:** As noted above, humulene is among the more COA-stable terpenes. Unlike ocimene (which degrades rapidly before analysis), humulene values on cured flower COAs are a reasonably accurate representation. The `terpene_humulene` column, once populated, should provide useful data.
