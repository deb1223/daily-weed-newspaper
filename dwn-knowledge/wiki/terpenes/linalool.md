**HEADLINE:** Linalool is lavender's terpene — GABA-mediated calm that pairs with myrcene to anchor the sleep profile.

**SUMMARY:** Linalool is a monoterpenoid alcohol from lavender and cannabis — the sedating, anxiety-calming terpene that works via GABA and glutamate modulation. Above 0.5% it produces genuine sedation and anxiolytic effects; above 1% it anchors the sleep-profile combination with myrcene. Broad analgesic activity including neuropathic pain via a unique inhalation olfactory pathway.

**ARTICLE:**

## What It Is

D-Linalool is a monoterpenoid alcohol — distinguished from myrcene, limonene, and caryophyllene by its oxygen-containing hydroxyl group, which gives it different stability and metabolic properties. It is the primary active terpene in lavender (*Lavandula angustifolia*), and also found in basil, citrus (trace), and cannabis. Its aroma is floral, sweet, and slightly spicy — the lavender note in cannabis cultivars. It is among the top eight most abundant terpenes in North American cannabis chemovars (Giese et al., 2015 in Liktor-Busa et al., 2021).

## Threshold Data

- **<0.1%** — Aroma only. Floral, lavender note detectable; no pharmacological effect expected.
- **>0.1%** — Contributing. Mild calming effect; GABAergic background activity beginning.
- **>0.5%** — Noticeable. Genuine sedation and anxiety reduction. Plan for evening or nighttime use; linalool above this level is not a functional daytime terpene.
- **>1%** — Dominant. Deep sedative profile. Combined with myrcene >0.5%, this is the sleep-profile stack — both body and mind are quieted through distinct mechanisms.

*Note: Thresholds applied from DWN standard framework. Source papers do not establish these thresholds directly at cannabis COA percentages.*

## Effect Goals Served

**Primary:** sleep, pain (thermal, inflammatory, neuropathic — unusually broad coverage), anxiety (sedating — evening use)
**Secondary:** inflammation, nausea/opioid-reduction (post-surgical morphine decrease documented), anticonvulsant
**Not served:** focus, social, creativity, daytime-anxiety (linalool's anxiolytic effect brings sedation with it — it is the opposite of limonene's clean functional uplift; wrong tool for daytime anxiety goals)

## Pharmacology

**GABA/Glutamate Modulation — Primary Mechanism:** Sedation, anxiolysis, and anticonvulsant effects are attributed to modulation of GABA and glutamate neurotransmitter systems (Nunes et al., 2010, cited Russo 2011). Specifically: GABAA channel concentration-dependent antagonism (Leal-Cardoso et al., 2010; Li et al., 2020), decreased K⁺-stimulated glutamate release in mouse synaptosomes (Silva Brum et al., 2001), voltage-gated Na⁺ and Ca²⁺ channel modulation, and NMDA receptor non-competitive inhibition (Elisabetsky et al., 1999). This is a different primary mechanism from all other terpenes covered so far: myrcene (opioid/TRPV1), limonene (serotonin/A2A), caryophyllene (CB2).

**Sedation via Inhalation — Confirmed:** Sedating to mouse activity on inhalation (Buchbauer et al., 1991; Jirovetz et al., 1992; Musty et al., 1976). Highly potent at extremely low serum concentrations — 73% reduction in motility at 4.22 ng/mL (Russo, 2011). These serum levels are comparable to those of THC in humans — linalool is pharmacologically active at concentrations that would be achieved during normal cannabis inhalation.

**Cannabimimetic (CB1-Mediated — LaVigne et al., 2021):** Linalool produces all four cannabinoid tetrad behaviors (antinociception, hypothermia, catalepsy, hypolocomotion) — making it genuinely cannabimimetic, not just behaviorally sedating. Antinociception is fully blocked by rimonabant, confirming CB1 dependence. ERK activation in CB1-CHO cells is rimonabant-sensitive, but linalool shows little orthosteric CB1 competition — suggesting allosteric binding. No opioid receptor involvement detected in this model (no ERK activation in MOR-CHO cells; naloxone had no effect on linalool-induced ERK).

> **Sex difference — clinically relevant:** LaVigne et al. (2021) found linalool's interactions with cannabinoids are sex-specific. When combined with a CB1 agonist, males show greater potentiation with earlier onset; females show delayed and reduced additive effects. Hypolocomotion in males is CB1-mediated (rimonabant-reversed); in females it is A2a-mediated (istradefyllene-reversed). This is the only terpene in our dataset with documented sex-specific pharmacology. Worth noting for personalized Talk to Ziggy recommendations when sex/biological context is provided.

**Analgesic — Unusually Broad:**
- *Thermal pain:* Hot-plate analgesia confirmed (Peana et al., 2003, 2004a); local anaesthetic effect equal to procaine and menthol (Ghelardini et al., 1999; Re et al., 2000).
- *Inflammatory pain:* Formalin model (Katsuyama et al., 2015); capsaicin model (Sakurada et al., 2011).
- *Neuropathic pain:* Anti-allodynic in SNL model (Berliocchi et al., 2009); anti-hyperalgesic in fibromyalgia model (Nascimento et al., 2014); neuropathy-induced mechanical hyperalgesia (Katsuyama et al., 2012; Kuwahata et al., 2013). Lavender EO (30% linalool) effective via peripheral + central opioid and CB2 receptors (Donatello et al., 2020).
- Peripheral opioid involvement confirmed in several studies (naloxone-sensitive), though LaVigne found no opioid receptor involvement at systemic CB1 doses — likely route and dose-dependent.

**Unique Inhalation Pain Pathway (Tashiro et al., 2016):** Linalool delivered via inhalation produces antinociception through the olfactory bulb and hypothalamic orexin neurons — a neuroanatomical pathway distinct from systemic absorption. Critically, β-myrcene and β-pinene were *ineffective* via this inhalation route in the same study. This is linalool's unique pharmacological feature: inhalation is not just a bioavailability advantage, it activates a separate CNS pain pathway that oral or dermal administration cannot replicate.

**Morphine-Sparing:** Linalool synergizes with morphine to enhance antinociception against capsaicin and neuropathic allodynia (Sakurada et al., 2011; Kuwahata et al., 2013). Inhalation of lavender EO (presumed linalool) decreased post-surgical morphine usage vs. placebo (P=0.04) in gastric banding patients (Russo, 2011). Potential opioid-sparing application for chronic pain management.

**Anticonvulsant:** With limonene (Jones et al., 2010); antiglutamatergic and anticonvulsant directly (Elisabetsky et al., 1995); reduced seizures in pentylenetetrazole, picrotoxin, and strychnine models.

## Entourage Combinations

| Partner | Effect | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Myrcene (>0.5%) | Sleep-profile | Myrcene sedates the body (muscle relaxation, central sedation); linalool quiets the mind (GABA/glutamate); distinct additive mechanisms for deep sleep |
| Limonene | Anxiolytic with edge of uplift | Anticonvulsant together (Jones 2010); linalool's GABAergic depth + limonene's serotonergic lift — best for anxiety without full sedation |
| CBD | Anxiolytic + anticonvulsant stack | CBD + limonene + linalool cited as anxiolytic combination by Russo (2011); linalool reinforces CBD's GABA/glutamate effects |
| Caryophyllene | Pain stack (especially neuropathic) | BCP CB2 + linalool peripheral opioid/NMDA = different analgesic pathways; effective for complex pain profiles |
| THC (males > females) | Cannabimimetic amplification | Additive CB1 effects — males get meaningful potentiation; females show less predictable enhancement |

**What amplifies it:** Myrcene, CBD, high THC (especially in male biology per LaVigne).
**What counteracts it:** Terpinolene and high pinene reduce sedative effect; limonene partially lifts the sedation ceiling.

## Consumption Route

**Inhalation (flower, vape, concentrate):** Best route — and uniquely so. Inhalation is the only delivery method that activates the olfactory bulb → orexin neuron antinociception pathway (Tashiro et al., 2016). This adds a pain-relief mechanism entirely absent from oral/dermal routes. Additionally, effects occur at very low serum concentrations (4.22 ng/mL for motility reduction), making inhalation efficient for CNS delivery.

**Oral / Edibles:** Linalool is a monoterpenoid alcohol — more stable than simple hydrocarbon monoterpenes. Linalyl acetate (present in lavender-adjacent cannabis) hydrolyses to linalool in gastric secretions (Bickers et al., 2003), acting as a natural prodrug. The GABA/glutamate mechanism is systemic and compatible with oral delivery. Anti-inflammatory and analgesic effects have been demonstrated at oral doses. Oral edibles can deliver linalool's sedation and pain relief, though the unique olfactory pain pathway is lost.

**Dermal:** Absorption confirmed — 1,500 mg lavender EO massaged into skin produced 100 ng/mL plasma peak at 19 minutes, t½ 13.76 min (Jäger et al., 1992). Relevant for topicals (burns, psoriasis, local analgesia).

**Degradation:** As an alcohol, linalool has somewhat greater oxidative stability than simple hydrocarbon monoterpenes. Still volatile and heat-sensitive. Gamma-irradiated cannabis may show reduced linalool (Fan and Sokorai, 2002). Check COA date; pre-roll values approximate.
