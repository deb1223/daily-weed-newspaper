**HEADLINE:** Myrcene is the couch-lock terpene — the most abundant in cannabis and the engine behind sedative "indica" effects.

**SUMMARY:** Beta-myrcene dominates the terpene profile of most cannabis cultivars. At >0.5% it produces noticeable sedation and muscle relaxation; above 1% it drives the heavy body effect known as "couch-lock." It amplifies THC's sedative effects directly. Pain relief is real but dose-dependent and works best through inhalation/smoking — oral bioavailability is limited due to rapid liver metabolism.

**ARTICLE:**

## What It Is

Beta-myrcene (β-myrcene) is a monoterpene — two isoprene units — and typically the single most abundant terpene in cannabis. It's also found in hops (*Humulus lupulus*), where it's been used as a recognized sleep aid in Germany, mango, lemongrass, and bay leaf. Its aroma profile is earthy, musky, and slightly fruity — the smell most people associate with "dank" flower.

Myrcene is one of only three terpenes (alongside limonene and α-pinene) found in significant quantities across the majority of cannabis chemovars worldwide (Liktor-Busa et al., 2021). In "mostly indica" cultivars, myrcene co-dominates with limonene and α-pinene. Sativa-leaning cultivars tend to have more complex, varied terpene profiles.

## Threshold Data

- **<0.1%** — Aroma contribution only. No pharmacological effect expected at this level.
- **>0.1%** — Contributing level. Mild muscle relaxation may be present; earthy aroma is detectable.
- **>0.5%** — Noticeable effects. Expect sedation regardless of indica/sativa label. This is the threshold where myrcene starts driving a body-heavy, relaxed experience. If a product's COA shows myrcene >0.5%, plan your session accordingly — this is not a functional daytime terpene at this level.
- **>1%** — Dominant. Significant sedation and muscle relaxation. Combined with THC, this is the chemical driver of "couch-lock." At this concentration myrcene is running the show.

*Note: These thresholds apply to cannabis flower/concentrate in COA context. The research literature tested isolated myrcene at high mg/kg doses in animal models — cannabis % thresholds are derived from the standard DWN framework applied against sedation research.*

## Effect Goals Served

**Primary:** sleep, pain, inflammation, nausea
**Secondary:** muscle relaxation, anxiety (high-myrcene may reduce anxiety for some via sedation — but note this is blunting, not the targeted anxiolytic profile of limonene or pinene)
**Not served:** focus, creativity, social, daytime-anxiety (myrcene is counterproductive for these goals above 0.5%)

## Pharmacology

**Sedation:** Myrcene is a clinically recognized sedative in European herbal medicine, specifically in hops preparations. In mice, it acts as a muscle relaxant and potentiates barbiturate sleep time at higher doses (do Vale et al., 2002; Russo, 2011). Sedative effects observed via inhalation in animal models (Buchbauer et al., 1993).

**Analgesia:** Antinociceptive in animal hot-plate, writhing, and formalin tests (Rao et al., 1990; Lorenzetti et al., 1991; Paula-Freire et al., 2013). The pain relief is partially opioid-mediated — naloxone blocks the effect in several studies (Rao et al., 1990; Paula-Freire et al., 2016). Antiallodynic and anti-hyperalgesic in neuropathic pain models at 1–10 mg/kg oral over 2 weeks (Paula-Freire et al., 2016).

**Anti-inflammatory:** Reduces inflammation via prostaglandin E-2 (PGE-2) suppression (Lorenzetti et al., 1991). Blocks PGE2- and isoprenaline-induced hyperalgesia.

**Receptor targets:** Partially opioid system (naloxone-sensitive). Possible TRPV1 activation shown on rat channel (Jansen et al., 2019), but this has NOT been confirmed on human TRPV1 (Heblinski et al., 2020). Mechanism remains partially elusive — myrcene likely acts on multiple targets.

**Entourage with THC:** Russo (2011) hypothesizes myrcene + THC directly produces "couch-lock" — myrcene may lower blood-brain barrier resistance, potentiating THC's CNS effects. This is the most pharmacologically plausible explanation for why high-THC high-myrcene products hit harder and heavier than THC alone. Anti-anxiety and possible CBD/CBG synergy also noted (Russo, 2011, Table 2).

**Cancer/hepatic:** Blocks hepatic carcinogenesis by aflatoxin in animal models (De Oliveira et al., 1997). Non-mutagenic in Ames test.

## Entourage Combinations

| Partner | Effect | Why |
|---|---|---|
| THC | Amplifies sedation and body effect | Myrcene + THC = couch-lock; myrcene may enhance CNS penetration of THC |
| Linalool | Deep sedation + anxiolytic layer | Sleep profile; myrcene drives the body, linalool quiets the mind |
| Caryophyllene | Pain relief stack | Anti-inflammatory via different pathways (PGE-2 vs CB2); additive for pain/inflammation goals |
| Limonene (>0.3%) | Partial counterbalance | Limonene's serotonergic uplift may soften myrcene's heaviness; good for anxiety-adjacent pain where full sedation is unwanted |
| α-Pinene | Counteracts somewhat | Pinene is a bronchodilator and cognition enhancer; high pinene cuts into myrcene's sedation — daytime-anxiety profile deliberately uses low myrcene |

**What amplifies it:** THC, linalool, other sedative terpenes.
**What counteracts it:** α-Pinene, limonene, terpinolene — these are the "sativa" terpene stack specifically because they work against myrcene's heaviness.

## Consumption Note — CRITICAL FOR PRODUCT RECOMMENDATIONS

**Inhalation (flower, vape, concentrate):** Best route for myrcene's sedative effects. Myrcene is volatile and reaches the bloodstream rapidly via inhalation. Sedative effects in animal models were demonstrated via inhalation (Buchbauer et al., 1993). Fast onset.

**Oral/Edibles:** Myrcene undergoes significant first-pass liver metabolism (inhibits CYP3A, induces CYP2A). Oral bioavailability of unchanged myrcene is low — it degrades into metabolites including linalool and benzene compounds (Liktor-Busa et al., 2021). This means an edible with high-myrcene extract will behave very differently than flower with the same terpene profile. **Do not make myrcene-based effect predictions for edibles — the terpene is largely metabolized before reaching the CNS.** Pain effects were demonstrated at high oral doses in animal studies but translate poorly to consumer edible dosing.

**Degradation:** Myrcene is a monoterpene and is heat/oxidation-sensitive. Improperly stored or old flower loses myrcene rapidly. Always check COA date. Pre-roll myrcene content should be treated as approximate.
